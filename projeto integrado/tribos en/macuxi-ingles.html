Food

The Macuxi's diet combines agriculture, hunting, fishing and gathering natural foods. They grow manioc, corn, beans, sweet potatoes, pumpkin and yams, with an emphasis on manioc to make flour. Hunting and fishing provide protein, including deer, armadillos, pacas and freshwater fish. They also collect fruit, nuts, roots and wild vegetables. With contact with non-indigenous society, they adopted industrialized foods such as rice, beans, pasta and canned products.

Art

The Macuxi have a distinct culture that includes their own language, organization in malocas, agricultural economy and spiritual practices. Their culture involves traditional art, dance and music. Despite modern challenges, such as pressure on their lands, the Macuxi are committed to preserving their traditions and contributing to Brazil's cultural diversity.

Language

The Macuxi language, also called Makushi, is spoken by approximately 30,000 people in Brazil and Guyana. Belonging to the Carib family, it is crucial to the cultural identity of the Macuxi indigenous group. The preservation and use of the language is essential for passing on ancestral culture and knowledge. In addition to Macuxi, many speak Portuguese, the official language of Brazil, especially in interactions with non-indigenous society.

History


The history of the Macuxi is marked by different phases:

1. Villages: The Portuguese colonial occupation of the Rio Branco region in the 18th century was aimed at military strategy. The Portuguese established Fort São Joaquim to protect their lands from possible Spanish and Dutch invasions. They sought to settle the indigenous Macuxi, offering gifts and using military tactics to convince them. However, these attempts met with little success, and a revolt in 1790 marked the end of the official settlement policy.

2. Extractivism: In the 19th century, the exploitation of rubber, caucho and balata in the forests of the lower Rio Branco had an impact on the Macuxi. The Indians were recruited as a labor force for extractivism, mainly in the area of the Rio Negro. Private enterprises dominated interethnic relations, weakening the official indigenist policy.

3. cattle ranching: extractivism financed the growth of cattle ranching on the upper Rio Branco, and cattle ranching favored the forced recruitment of indigenous labor. The Indians were exploited and forced to work, with no instances to protect them from slavery at the hands of white entrepreneurs. Indigenous migrations were often caused by the advance of cattle ranching.

Today, the Macuxi face challenges in demarcating their lands, including urban areas and non-indigenous farms, such as the Raposa Serra do Sol indigenous land. This represents a significant impasse related to indigenous rights in contemporary Brazil.

Food

  • Agriculture and gathering: Agriculture is essential for the Macuxi, with manioc, maize, beans, sweet potatoes, pumpkin and yams grown. Manioc is especially valued and used to make flour, a staple food. In addition, they collect fruit, nuts, roots and wild vegetables in the wild.



  • Hunting and fishing: Hunting plays an important role, with deer, armadillos, pacas, birds and other wild animals as sources of protein in the diet. Fishing in local rivers and lakes provides freshwater fish such as tucunaré, pacu and surubim.



  • Contemporary influences: With contact with non-indigenous society, the Macuxi have incorporated industrialized foods such as rice, beans, pasta and canned products into their diets, reflecting the influence of modern eating practices.



The Macuxi diet is a balance between ancient traditions and adaptations to changes in food availability.

Art

The Macuxi culture is a rich and distinctive part of Brazil's mosaic of indigenous traditions. This indigenous group, with a population of approximately 30,000 people in Brazil and Guyana, is known for its deep-rooted traditions in its language, social organization, economy and spirituality.

    Characteristics in Indigenous Art:

  • Essential Traditions: The Macuxi have deep-rooted traditions, including a distinct language, social organization in malocas and an economy based on farming, hunting and fishing. Cassava is an essential part of their diet.
  • Art and Spirituality: Artistic practices such as painting and handicrafts are part of their culture. They perform dances and rituals to honor nature spirits and ancestors.
  • Contemporary challenges: They face pressure on their lands due to the exploitation of natural resources and contact with non-indigenous society. However, they seek to preserve their traditions, contributing to Brazil's cultural diversity.

Territorial area


The Macuxi, an ethnic group of Carib origin, inhabit the region between the Branco and Rupununi rivers, currently divided between Brazil and Guyana. The Macuxi are part of a larger ethnic group called the Pemon, which includes the Taurepang, Arekuna, Kamarakoto, Arakaio and Patamona, among others.

In 2004, it was estimated that there were around 19,000 Macuxi in Brazil, with half of the population living in Guyana. Their territory extends over approximately 30,000 to 40,000 km² and comprises grassland areas to the south and mountainous regions to the north, where the forest is denser.

The Macuxi population lives in several villages and small isolated dwellings, with approximately 140 villages in Brazil and 50 in Guyana, according to data from 2004. This spatial distribution has been constant throughout history, remaining unchanged since the 18th century, when the first historical records of the Rio Branco valley region are available.

The Macuxi culture is deeply linked to its relationship with the environment, varying according to the areas of grassland and forest in their territory. Their traditions include spiritual practices, art and rituals that honor nature and the ancestors. The Macuxi language is a fundamental element of their identity.

Language

The Macuxi language, the most widely spoken of the Caribbean languages, is used by around 30,000 people in Brazil and Guyana. It is also known by different spellings, such as Makushi, Makusi, Makuxi, Macusi, Macussi, Teweya and Teueia.

This language plays a fundamental role in the cultural identity of the Macuxi, belonging to the Carib linguistic family and being distinct from the languages spoken by other indigenous ethnic groups in Brazil. The preservation and continued use of the Macuxi language is essential for passing on their culture, traditions and ancestral knowledge from one generation to the next. In addition to Macuxi, many Macuxi also speak Portuguese, the official language of Brazil, mainly in interactions with non-indigenous society.
